In November 2025, the author wrote the first version of this answer. Nearly three years later, the system was updated, reflecting changes in personal knowledge management and iteration of notebook structures.

The core issue for many people when managing notes or files is **not knowing how to build an effective folder or notebook system**. Random creation of folders and inconsistent naming conventions lead to chaos. Without structure, retrieving information later feels like finding a needle in a haystack.

This article explores how to construct a lifelong notebook and file system with Evernote as an example.

## 1: Build Once, Use for Life

The system is divided into three parts:

- **Inbox system**: Collect all inputs into a single inbox to prevent scattered information.
- **Main knowledge base with one classification dimension**: Categories are divided into Work, Study, Life, and Hobbies.
- **Archive system**: Old but potentially useful materials are archived rather than deleted.

## 1.1: Inbox Principle

Inbox serves as the universal entry point, similar to an email inbox. By centralizing collection, you process in batches rather than interrupting focus with every new piece of input. Regularly clearing the inbox ensures a steady flow of organized knowledge.

## 2: Four Guiding Principles

- **Single classification dimension (MECE principle)**: Avoid overlapping categories; one clear system simplifies placement and retrieval. For example, Wikipedia uses subject/nature as its main classification.
- **Numbering system (Dewey Decimal adaptation)**: Use decimal-based numbering to ensure order and expansion flexibility.
- **Naming rules**: A notebook name includes number, category, and specific title.
- **Workflow (GTD)**: Notes flow from inbox → processing → knowledge base → archive.

## 3: Step-by-Step Setup

1. Create a mind map of focus areas
2. Build corresponding notebooks and stacks in Evernote (or folders in file systems)
3. Iterate continuously; no system is perfect from the start

## 3.1: Advantages

- Automatic order via numbering
- Scalable to thousands of notes
- Simple and effective across career and life transitions

## 4: Summary

The system combines **Wikipedia-style classification**, **Dewey numbering**, and **GTD flow**, producing a flexible, lifelong framework for managing knowledge.

## Additional Notes (2025 versions and updates)

- Start by collecting freely, then structure once volume grows
- Use **iterative development**—refine structure over time
- Avoid only collecting without learning; schedule weekly reviews
- Evernote acts as an **external brain**, mirroring internal knowledge
- Distinguish between **theoretical knowledge systems** (broad storage) and **application systems** (focused, for growth)

The final takeaway:
Knowledge systems should not just be archives. They must enable **instant retrieval, active application, and personal growth**. The goal is not simply building a system, but making it serve as your second brain.
